A guest's flight is delayed, they land at midnight, and they call your front desk to ask if they can still check in. Nobody picks up. By morning they've either booked elsewhere or left a one-star review about the "phone that rings forever." For a property with 5 to 20 rooms, that single missed call can be the difference between a full weekend and an empty room — and the caller who gives up often just books through an online travel agency instead, handing over a 15% to 30% commission you didn't have to pay. ([Cloudbeds](https://www.cloudbeds.com/online-travel-agencies/commissions/))

So the real question isn't whether to cover the phone. It's *how*. Here are the three realistic options, weighed honestly.

## What each one actually costs

**Part-time night staff.** A hotel night auditor or overnight front-desk person runs roughly $15 to $22 an hour depending on the role and your region. ([PayScale](https://www.payscale.com/research/US/Job=Night_Auditor%2C_Hotel/Hourly_Rate), [Glassdoor](https://www.glassdoor.com/Salaries/front-desk-night-auditor-salary-SRCH_KO0,24.htm)) Covering an eight-hour overnight shift, seven nights a week, is about 56 hours — and one person can't work every night, so you're really staffing two people. Add payroll taxes and you're looking at $4,000 to $6,000 a month for full overnight coverage. For a 6-room B&B, that's often more than the rooms earn overnight.

**Answering service.** A traditional live-operator service typically runs $135 to $450 a month, usually billed against a bucket of minutes with per-minute overages of $0.75 to $2.00 once you go over. Watch for setup fees, after-hours premiums, and per-transfer charges. ([Nextiva](https://www.nextiva.com/blog/answering-service-cost.html), [Answering365](https://www.answering365.com/blog/answering-service-cost-2026-pricing-guide/))

**AI booking agent.** AI-powered phone services generally fall in the $50 to $300 a month range, with the higher end reflecting stronger language handling and the ability to connect to your booking system. ([Nextiva](https://www.nextiva.com/blog/answering-service-cost.html)) Purpose-built hotel versions that write directly into your property management system can cost more, but they don't charge per minute, so a busy night doesn't spike the bill. (If you're new to what an "agent" even means here, [this piece](/blog/the-agentic-wave-is-not-just-for-tech/) is the one-minute version.)

## Coverage: the overnight hours and the check-in rush

This is where staffing and machines separate.

An **AI agent** and an **answering service** both cover 24/7 at the same price — 3 a.m. costs the same as 3 p.m. The difference shows up during a *surge*: four cars pulling in at 4 p.m. while two guests call at once. A single answering-service operator handles calls one at a time. An AI agent can take every call in parallel, which matters far more for a small property than raw hours do.

**Night staff** cover the phone *and* the door — someone is physically there for a lockout or a lost key. No software does that.

## Booking accuracy and upselling

An **answering service** operator is a generalist covering many clients. They can take a message reliably, but they usually can't see live room availability, so most services just relay the request for you to handle in the morning — by which point the room may be gone.

An **AI agent** connected to your booking system checks real availability, quotes the right rate, and confirms the reservation on the call. It can also handle simple upsells consistently — the late checkout, the breakfast add-on, the better room — because it never forgets to ask and never gets shy about it. That said, an AI agent is only as accurate as the calendar it reads; if your availability isn't synced, it will confidently book a room you don't have.

**Night staff** can do all of this and read the guest, but only when they're awake, on shift, and not already helping someone at the desk.

## Where each one leaves you exposed

Be clear-eyed about the failure modes:

- **Special requests** — an anniversary setup, an accessibility need, a pet question. An AI agent should recognize it's out of its depth and hand off; an answering service will take a message; only staff can actually *solve* it in the moment.
- **Complaints** — an upset guest at midnight wants a person, not a cheerful bot. This is the clearest case for a human, or at minimum an instant escalation path.
- **Safety issues** — a smoke smell, a medical scare, an intruder. This must route to a real person immediately, every time. If a tool can't guarantee that, it's disqualified for overnight, full stop.

The pattern is the same one a [40-seat restaurant](/blog/one-friday-night-at-a-40-seat-restaurant-what-the/) found: the machine handles the routine volume, and a well-drawn line sends the human stuff to a human.

## How to decide this week

Pull your phone records for the last 90 days and count two things: how many calls came in *after hours*, and how many went unanswered. If the answer is "a handful, and they're mostly routine booking questions," an AI booking agent tied to your calendar is the cheapest way to stop the bleed — as long as you set a hard rule that complaints and safety calls ring a real phone. If your overnight volume is high or you genuinely need a body on-site for lockouts, staff earn their cost. And if you just need messages taken and can wait until morning, an answering service is the low-effort middle.

Whatever you choose, run it as a 60-day trial against that same call log, and check one number: how many after-hours callers ended up booked with *you* instead of a competitor.

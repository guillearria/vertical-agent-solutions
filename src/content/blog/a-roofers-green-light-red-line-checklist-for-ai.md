When a hailstorm rolls through, your phone doesn't ring politely one call at a time. It floods. And you're forty feet up on a steep-slope tear-off with your hands full, watching the missed-call number climb. Every one of those you don't catch tonight is a homeowner dialing the next roofer on Google tomorrow.

That's the exact gap an AI phone agent is built to fill — a tool that answers, texts, and books around the clock. If you want the plain-English version of what these agents are before you read on, start with [our primer on the agentic wave](/blog/the-agentic-wave-is-not-just-for-tech/). This post is narrower: a sorting list for a small crew deciding what to hand the machine and what to keep in human hands.

Why it matters that someone answers fast: a widely cited study out of MIT and *Harvard Business Review* found that businesses responding to a new lead within five minutes were 100 times more likely to reach the person and 21 times more likely to move them into an actual sales conversation than those who waited 30 minutes. During a storm surge, "within five minutes" is impossible for a human crew. It's trivial for software.

## Green light: hand these over

These are low-risk, high-volume, and mostly scripted. An agent handles them cleanly.

- **After-hours and storm-surge lead capture.** Name, address, phone, "what happened," photos if they can send them. The agent logs the lead and confirms someone will call to schedule. This is the single biggest win.
- **Booking free inspections.** Reading your calendar, offering open slots, and confirming an appointment is exactly the kind of repetitive scheduling software does well — the same pattern that keeps [a landscaping company above water during its spring phone flood](/blog/can-an-ai-answering-service-keep-up-with-a/).
- **Basic FAQs.** Service area, whether you do metal vs. shingle, roughly how long a typical inspection takes, are you licensed and insured. Facts that don't change from job to job.
- **Appointment reminders and reschedules.** "Confirm, change, or cancel" texts cut no-shows without a person lifting a finger.
- **Claim-status check-ins and review requests.** A polite text — "Any word from your adjuster?" or "Mind leaving us a review?" — that you'd otherwise forget to send while you're on a roof.

## Handle with guardrails

Fine for an agent *if* you set firm limits. Vague ballparks and rough timelines are where roofers get in trouble, so script these tightly.

- **Rough job sequencing, not prices.** "Repairs usually take a day; full replacements one to three" is fine. A dollar figure is not (see below).
- **Deposits or scheduling fees** — only if your process already uses a fixed, published amount. Never a percentage of an unquoted job.
- **Warranty questions** — the agent can state your standard terms verbatim, then hand off anything beyond a straight readout.

## Red line: keep these human

Cross these and you risk a lawsuit, a voided contract, or a furious customer. No agent should touch them.

- **Quoting a roof replacement price sight-unseen.** You haven't seen the deck, the layers, or the pitch. A number over the phone becomes the number they expect — and dispute.
- **Promising insurance coverage or "we'll get it approved."** In Texas, Florida, and a growing list of states, a roofer who negotiates or "handles" a homeowner's claim is illegally acting as an unlicensed public adjuster — a real enforcement risk with fines and voided contracts attached. Your agent must never say a claim will be covered or offer to fight the insurer.
- **Promising code compliance or engineering opinions.** "It'll pass inspection" is a judgment call for a person who's on-site and licensed.
- **Angry warranty disputes and leaks.** A ceiling stain and a scared homeowner need a human voice and a truck, not a chatbot. Route these straight to you.

## Before you switch it on

Do one thing this week: write down your five most common storm-season calls and mark each one green, yellow, or red using the list above. That single page becomes the script you hand any vendor — and the line they're never allowed to cross. Then test the agent by calling your own number after hours and trying to trick it into quoting a price. If it holds the line, you've got a night-shift lead-catcher. If it doesn't, you've caught the problem before a customer does.

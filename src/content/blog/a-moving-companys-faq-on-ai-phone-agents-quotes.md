Movers lose jobs in the gap between a customer's call and a callback. When you're under a truck's tailgate wrapping a sofa, the phone rings, and the person on the other end is dialing three other companies too. An AI phone and text agent is built for exactly that gap. Here are the questions movers actually ask about them.

If you want the ground-level explanation of what an "AI agent" even is, start with our [plain-English primer](/blog/the-agentic-wave-is-not-just-for-tech/) and come back. This piece assumes you have the gist.

## What can it actually do when someone calls or texts?

The reliable work is intake. A good agent answers on the first ring, day or night, and collects the details every estimate depends on: origin and destination ZIP codes, home size (studio, two-bedroom, four-bedroom house), stairs or elevator, preferred dates, and whether there are special items like a piano or a gun safe. It logs all of it into your lead system in a clean, consistent format — no scribbled sticky notes lost by Monday.

This matters because speed decides who wins. Vendors that reach out first tend to book the job, and plenty of movers are slow: one industry write-up citing SmartMoving data reported that most quote requests don't get a response within five minutes. An agent that never sleeps closes that window.

## Can it give the customer a price?

A ballpark, yes. A binding price, no — and that line is not optional.

The agent can quote a range based on your rate card: "A two-bedroom move across town usually runs $X to $Y, depending on final volume and access." That's enough to keep a shopper on the hook without a human touching the call.

What it must not do is issue a **binding estimate**. Under federal rules, a binding estimate locks in the total cost and has to be in writing, based on a real look at what's being moved. There's also the **110% rule** on interstate moves: for a non-binding estimate, you can't collect more than 110% of the quoted amount at delivery. Both of these are promises with legal weight. A price guaranteed sight-unseen over the phone is how movers get into disputes. Let the agent give a range and route the real number through your normal survey-and-estimate process.

## Can it book the survey?

This is where it earns its keep. The agent can offer either an **in-home estimate** or a **video survey** — the customer walks their phone through each room while software or an estimator builds the inventory. Video surveys skip the estimator's drive time and the customer's two-hour interruption, which is why many shoppers prefer them for smaller moves. An in-home visit still ranks as the most accurate option for complex or large homes, and a fully loaded in-home estimate can cost a mover $75–$150 in time and fuel, so steering simple jobs to video is genuinely useful.

The agent offers open slots, books the one the customer picks, and confirms it. For a busy front office, that's the same relief a [landscaping company gets during its spring phone flood](/blog/can-an-ai-answering-service-keep-up-with-a/).

## What about deposits, reminders, and reschedules?

All fair game, because they're rule-based:

- Text a secure deposit link and confirm when it's paid.
- Send survey and move-day reminders to cut no-shows.
- Reschedule a survey when a customer's date slips, then update the calendar.
- Follow up with quiet leads a day or two later.

None of this requires judgment. It requires doing the same reliable thing every time — which is what software is good at.

## What should it never handle?

Draw a bright line around anything with money, liability, or emotion on it:

- **Binding price commitments** and final estimates — human, always.
- **Damage or loss claims.** A customer whose dresser got gouged needs a person, not a chatbot.
- **Injury or property-damage calls** during a move — these are liability events; escalate immediately.
- **Angry or complex disputes.** The agent should recognize the tone and hand off fast.

The skill isn't answering everything. It's knowing what to pass up the chain — the same sorting a pest control team relies on when [their booking agent catches some calls and hands others to a human](/blog/a-spring-week-at-a-pest-control-company-what-the/).

## Does it replace my estimator or sales rep?

No. It replaces the missed call and the 9 p.m. voicemail. Your estimator still runs the survey and closes the sale; the agent just makes sure that estimator is talking to a booked, qualified lead instead of chasing tag.

## A low-risk way to try it

Point it at one narrow job first: after-hours and missed calls only. Have it capture lead details, quote a range, and book a video survey — nothing more. Run it for a month, then read the transcripts. You'll see exactly which calls it handled cleanly and which ones you'd rather keep for a person, and you can widen its role from there.

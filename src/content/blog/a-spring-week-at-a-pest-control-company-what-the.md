Spring is when the phone stops behaving. Pest control companies routinely see a jump once the weather warms — one industry forecast for 2026 predicted one of the busiest pest seasons in years, with rising activity across ants, rodents, roaches, spiders, and wasps. Queens wake up, colonies expand, and homeowners who ignored a few ants in March start calling in a panic by May.

So let's follow one made-up-but-typical company — call it Ridgeline Pest — through a single busy week. Ridgeline runs three trucks and one office manager, Dana, who cannot answer the phone while she's also dispatching technicians. This spring, Ridgeline added an AI voice agent to pick up when Dana can't. Here's what it actually did, day by day, and where it stepped aside.

If "AI agent" is a fuzzy term for you, here's a [plain-language primer](/blog/the-agentic-wave-is-not-just-for-tech/) before we start.

## Monday: the calls that used to hit voicemail

The first warm Monday brings a flood. By mid-morning Dana is on the road with a technician's paperwork problem, and eleven calls come in.

In the old setup, most of those would have gone to voicemail — and voicemail is where jobs die. Studies of small-business phone habits consistently find that a large share of callers who hit voicemail simply hang up and call the next company instead. One widely-cited analysis put the share of unanswered business calls above 60%.

The agent answers all eleven. Nine are routine: an ant problem, two wasp nests, a "do you treat spiders?" question. It captures each caller's name, address, and the pest, then books a visit and reads back the appointment time. Two callers just want pricing, which we'll get to.

## Tuesday: booking the boring, profitable stuff

Recurring service is the backbone of a pest control business, and it's exactly the kind of predictable task an AI agent handles well. Three existing customers call to renew their quarterly plan. The agent confirms the address on file, offers the next open slots, and books them — including the detail that trips up a lot of first-time technicians: **the gate code**. It captures "side gate, code 4-1-8-2, dog is friendly but loud" into the appointment notes so the tech isn't stuck in a driveway.

It also collects the small things that make a visit go smoothly: whether it's a front or back entry, where the previous bait stations are, and a callback number in case the crew is running late.

## Wednesday: routing the urgent one

A restaurant calls at 7:40 a.m. about a rodent sighting before a health inspection. This is where routing matters. The agent recognizes "rodents" plus "commercial" plus urgency, so instead of dropping it into the next available residential slot three days out, it flags the call as priority and texts Dana immediately with the details. Dana calls back within twenty minutes and squeezes in a same-day visit.

The agent didn't decide how bad the infestation was. It just recognized the words that mean "a human should look at this now" and made sure a human did.

## Thursday: the honest limits

Thursday is a good day to see what the agent *won't* do — and shouldn't.

- **Diagnosing severity over the phone.** A caller describes "some bugs with wings" near a windowsill. Termites? Flying ants? A swarm or a stray? That judgment belongs to a licensed technician who can see them. The agent books an inspection and doesn't guess.
- **Custom commercial contracts.** A property manager wants pricing for eight buildings with different square footage and a shared billing arrangement. The agent takes the details and hands the negotiation to Dana. No quoting a number it can't stand behind.
- **Anything needing a license.** Questions about specific chemicals, safety around pets and kids, or treatment plans get routed to a certified tech. The agent's job is to book the visit, not to give the advice.

This is the same division of labor other trades are landing on — an [AI receptionist earns its keep on volume and handoffs](/blog/ai-receptionist-vs-answering-service-vs-a-new-hire/), not on judgment calls.

## Friday: the pricing FAQ

Friday's calls are half price-shoppers. The agent answers the questions with clear, pre-approved answers Dana wrote: "A standard one-time ant treatment starts at $X; quarterly plans are $Y a visit; wasp nest removal depends on location, so we quote that on site." For anything outside the script — a discount request, a bundled deal — it offers a callback rather than improvising.

## What the week added up to

No missed jobs to voicemail. Recurring visits booked with the gate codes attached. One urgent commercial call routed to a human fast. And a clear, boring boundary around everything that needs a license or a judgment call.

If you run a similar shop, don't automate the whole phone at once. Pick one thing to start: write down the five pricing questions you answer most, and the exact words you'd want a stand-in to say. That script is the foundation an AI agent — or a new hire — actually needs.
